What Is Dry Eye?

Dry eye is perhaps the single most common eye condition faced by our patients. Dry eye occurs when the eye does not produce enough tears, or does not have enough quality tears, to keep the surface of the cornea moist.

Causes

Dry eye causes a wide range of symptoms. It is the most common cause of tearing or watering; classically, tearing related to dry eye is exacerbated by with exposure to cold, wind, or sun. Dry eye can be painful and may feel like a dull gritty, or sandy, sensation at times, and like a sharp stabbing pain at other times. Dry eye also causes transient blurry vision, especially blurring of vision after moments of focused visual activity such as reading, TV watching, or driving. Redness may also be a sign of dry eye.

Treatment Options

There is no cure for dry eye. Most cases are mild and can be managed simply with conservative treatments:

  • Artifical tears four times a day; preservative free artificial tears if needed more than four times per day.
  • Lubricating gel or ointment at bedtime.
  • Warm compresses using a hot towel, or a microwaveable eye mask, such as a Bruder eye mask, for five minutes twice a day.
  • Omega-3 fatty acid supplements.
  • Humidifier on in bedroom at night.
  • Directing car heating vents away from face.
  • Limiting screen time.

Many patients ask us which brand of artificial tears to use. We advise patients that artificial tears are a personal preference, much like a choice of facial lotion or hand moisturizer, and we cannot predict which brands may suit you best or be at a suitable price point. For mild cases of dry eye in younger patients who have not had eye surgery, and don’t take glaucoma drops, any of the readily available drop formulations on drug store shelves should be suitable including: Refresh Optive Advanced, Systane Ultra, Systane Complete, or Hydrasense. Moderate to severe cases, especially in patients who use glaucoma drops, or have had laser eye surgery or cataract surgery, would benefit from preservative free artificial tears. A common combination we recommend is Hylo drops four times a day with Ocunox gel at night; both products are available in drugstores, and at our office. Hylo Gel, Hylo Dual, Hyabak, Thealoz, and Thealoz Duo are other well known preservative free options. Many patients also ask us why artificial tears are not covered by OHIP or their insurance plans; again, analogous to facial moisturizers or hand creams, lubricating eye drops are not covered.

Moderate to Severe Cases

Moderate to severe cases of dry eye can be treated with medical management including:

  • Topical Restasis eye drops.
  • Topical Xiidra eye drops.
  • A course of low dose oral antibiotics.

Topical Restasis and Xiidra are slow-acting medications intended for long term use, only in patients with dry eye refractory to conservative management. Very severe cases of intractable dry eye may benefit from serum tears and/or punctal plugs or punctal cautery.

There are additional, non insured, treatments available for dry eye such as mechanical expression of the eyelid glands, Lipiflow therapy, or intense pulsed light laser therapy. We do not perform or offer any of these ancillary treatments, however, many of our Optometry colleagues do, and, if you do not have an Optometrist, we can refer you to an Optometrist specializing in dry eye if you would like to explore one of these therapies.

Rarely, some cases of dry eye feature pain out of proportion to findings and do not resolve with any of the treatments described above. In these cases, we suspect that the pain is neuropathic, or nerve-based, in origin and we enlist the help of other specialists such as Neurologists to help manage your symptoms with oral medications.
